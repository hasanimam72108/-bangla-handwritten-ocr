# Chapter 2: Literature Review

## 2.1 Evolution of Optical Character Recognition (OCR)
The development of Optical Character Recognition systems has historically progressed from template-matching techniques to sophisticated deep learning architectures. Traditional OCR systems for Latin scripts relied heavily on Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) combined with handcrafted feature extraction methods. The paradigm shifted with the introduction of Convolutional Recurrent Neural Networks (CRNNs), which combined Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for image feature extraction with Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) for sequence modeling, optimized using Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC) loss. CRNNs became the de facto standard for sequence recognition tasks, providing high efficiency and accuracy for printed and handwritten text.

## 2.2 OCR for the Bengali Script
Unlike English, Bengali is a complex script consisting of basic characters, modifiers (dependent vowels), and conjuncts formed by combining two or more consonants. Initial research on Bengali OCR predominantly focused on printed text using dataset resources like CMATERdb. Approaches involved segmenting words into individual characters or graphemes, followed by classification using Support Vector Machines (SVMs) or multi-layer perceptrons.

The transition to unconstrained offline handwriting recognition required larger datasets. The BanglaLekha-Isolated dataset provided individual characters, but line-level recognition remained largely unexplored until the introduction of the BN-HTRd (Bangla Handwritten Text Recognition) benchmark dataset. Comprising over 100,000 words forming thousands of lines sourced from BBC News articles, BN-HTRd presented a modern challenge. Baseline evaluations on this dataset primarily utilized CRNN-CTC architectures, yielding moderate success but struggling with the extreme variability of cursive strokes and the sheer volume of morphological combinations.

## 2.3 Transformers in Vision and Language
The introduction of the Transformer architecture fundamentally altered natural language processing (NLP) and subsequently computer vision. Vision Transformers (ViT) divide images into fixed-size patches, linearly embed them, and process them through self-attention layers, capturing global context more effectively than CNNs. 

In 2021, Microsoft introduced TrOCR (Transformer-based Optical Character Recognition), which discarded convolutions entirely. TrOCR pairs a pre-trained image transformer (e.g., ViT or DeiT) as the encoder with a pre-trained text transformer (e.g., RoBERTa) as the decoder. This encoder-decoder framework approaches OCR as an image-to-text generation task. TrOCR achieved state-of-the-art accuracy on the IAM Handwriting dataset by leveraging large-scale pre-training on printed documents before fine-tuning on handwritten data.

## 2.4 Research Gap
While TrOCR excels in English handwriting, its application to Bengali introduces significant friction. The decoder (`microsoft/trocr-base-handwritten`) is fundamentally English-biased. When applied to Bengali, the language model struggles because it lacks prior knowledge of Bengali syntax, phonetics, and grammar. Furthermore, existing literature rarely addresses the engineering challenges of applying such large models in computationally constrained environments (e.g., Kaggle limits) or the practical difficulties of aligning massive word-level datasets into line-level training pairs. This project bridges this gap by engineering a robust pipeline that adapts the English-centric TrOCR model to the BN-HTRd dataset, handling strict dataset alignment, custom tokenization overrides, and rigorous hardware optimization.
